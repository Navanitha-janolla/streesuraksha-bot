In India, several laws protect women's safety, including the Protection of Women from Domestic Violence Act, 2005, the Dowry Prohibition Act, 1961, and the Sexual Harassment of Women at Workplace Act, 2013
Police’ and ‘Public Order’ are State subjects under the Seventh Schedule to the Constitution of India. State Governments are thus responsible for safety and security of the citizens including women and girls.
However, safety and security of women and children in the country is utmost  priority  for  the  Government. The Ministry of Women and Child Development has been administering various special laws relating to women such as the Protection of Women from Domestic Violence Act, 2005; Dowry Prohibition Act, 1961; Indecent Representation of Women (Prohibition) Act, 1986; the Sexual Harassment of Women at Workplace (Prevention, Prohibition and Redressal) Act, 2013 and the Prohibition of Child Marriage Act, 2006. The said Ministry is also administering the Juvenile Justice (care and protection of children) Act, 2015, the Commissions for Protection of Child Rights Act, 2005 and the Protection of Children from Sexual Offences Act, 2012.The Criminal Law (Amendments), Act 2013 was enacted for effective legal deterrence against sexual offences. Further, the Criminal Law (Amendment) Act, 2018 was enacted to prescribe even more stringent penal provisions including death penalty for rape of a girl below the age of 12 years. The Act also inter-alia mandates completion of investigation and trials within 2 months each.

Legal Protections for Women in India:
India has established several laws aimed at safeguarding women's rights and ensuring their safety:
The Dowry Prohibition Act, 1961: Prohibits the giving or receiving of dowry, aiming to eliminate dowry-related violence.​
AP News
The Sexual Harassment of Women at Workplace (Prevention, Prohibition and Redressal) Act, 2013 (PoSH Act): Mandates a safe working environment for women and requires organizations to address complaints of sexual harassment. ​
Kelp
The Protection of Women from Domestic Violence Act, 2005: Provides protection to women from physical, sexual, emotional, and economic abuse within the household.​
The Criminal Law (Amendment) Act, 2013: Enacted to strengthen the legal framework against sexual offenses, introducing stricter punishments for crimes such as rape and sexual assault. ​
The Maternity Benefit Act, 1961: Ensures paid maternity leave and other benefits to women employees. ​

Statistical Overview:
Despite legal measures, crimes against women remain a concern. In 2022, over 440,000 cases of crimes against women were reported, averaging 51 cases every hour. However, some reports indicate a declining trend in certain areas. ​

International Women’s Rights
Globally, women’s rights are protected by key international treaties:
CEDAW (Convention on the Elimination of All Forms of Discrimination Against Women) – UN treaty ratified by India in 1993.
Universal Declaration of Human Rights (UDHR) – Emphasizes gender equality.
Sustainable Development Goal 5 – Achieve gender equality and empower all women and girls by 2030.
Women empowerment means freeing women from all forms of discrimination—social, economic, political, caste, and gender. It allows them to make independent life choices and ensures parity, not patriarchy.
Human Rights: Women must have the freedom to express thoughts and make decisions confidently.
Social Empowerment: Promotes gender equality in all life spheres.
Educational Empowerment: Equips women with knowledge, skills, and awareness of their rights.
Economic Empowerment: Encourages financial independence and livelihood ownership.
Legal Empowerment: Requires strong legal structures to protect and empower women.
Political Empowerment: Ensures women's participation in governance and political decision-making.
